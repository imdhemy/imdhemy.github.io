In tech teams, reviews happen all the time. Not just code reviews, but design feedback, product discussions, and
sometimes even how we interact with each other. Whether it's a pull request, a strategy doc, or a decision in a meeting,
how we give and take feedback affects everything. It shapes how we work, how we feel about the team, and how good the
product ends up being.

If you want to build a healthy team culture, showing empathy, being kind, and staying respectful during reviews is not
a "nice to have". It's essential. Studies show that teams
with [psychological safety](https://rework.withgoogle.com/print/guides/5721312655835136/) perform better. People speak
up more, suggest ideas, and aren't afraid to admit mistakes. And that’s exactly what we need to grow and innovate.

In this post, I’ll share ideas and examples from real situations, some well-known frameworks, and things I’ve learned
along the way. The goal is simple: let’s make every review a constructive one.

## Why it matters: It’s about people first

When someone shares their work and asks for feedback, they’re showing vulnerability. And the way we respond makes a huge
difference. If the feedback feels like an attack, they shut down. If it feels like a conversation, they open up. That’s
when the real improvement starts.

Google’s [Project Aristotle](https://rework.withgoogle.com/print/guides/5721312655835136/) found that psychological
safety was the number one factor in high-performing teams. When people feel safe, they ask questions, admit what they
don’t know, and bring better ideas. Without safety, teams just go quiet. Or worse, they get defensive and stop growing.

Also, remember that we all come with different experiences. What seems "obvious" to one person might not be to someone
else. So we need to approach reviews with humility. Instead of saying "this is wrong", ask "what was the idea behind
this?" That small shift builds trust.

Giving feedback isn’t about being right, it’s about being helpful. It’s about supporting each other to make the work
better. This mindset applies whether you’re reviewing code, commenting on a design, or responding to a team member’s
suggestion in a meeting.

## Code reviews: More than just correctness

Code reviews are probably the most common type of feedback in tech. They help improve code quality, prevent bugs, and
share knowledge. But they can easily become a source of frustration. When the tone is harsh or the comments feel
personal, people start dreading reviews instead of learning from them.

Here are some ways to make code reviews more constructive:

* **Let tools do their job.** Use linters and formatters to handle style issues. Focus your comments on logic, clarity,
  and design.
* **Avoid blaming language.** Instead of saying "you missed this", say "this line might cause a null pointer exception".
* **Ask, don’t command.** "Could we extract this into a helper method?" is much more inviting than "extract this".
* **Give examples.** If you suggest a change, show how it could look. It saves time and avoids confusion.
* **Recognize good work.** If you see something well done, say it. "Nice use of early return here" can go a long way.
* **Think about timing and context.** Are you reviewing a hotfix or a long-term feature? Adjust your level of feedback
  accordingly.

When we focus too much on "correctness" without considering tone, timing, or context, we risk turning reviews into
debates. A better approach is to treat the review like a conversation, where both sides can learn and improve together.

## Beyond code: Design, docs, product, and people

Feedback isn’t limited to code. And often, the non-code reviews are just as important. Design critiques, roadmap
discussions, writing reviews, even team process feedback — all of these moments shape the product and the team.

**Design feedback:**
Design is subjective, but that doesn't mean anything goes. The best way to approach design reviews is by anchoring your
feedback in user needs. For example, if a button is hard to find, instead of saying "this is bad", say "would a larger
size help new users spot this faster?" Ask questions. Understand the design choices before suggesting changes. Remember,
designers are problem-solvers, not pixel decorators.

**Product and strategy discussions:**
These reviews can be tricky because they involve competing priorities. Maybe engineering wants stability, product wants
speed, and marketing wants features. The key here is alignment. Try to frame your feedback in terms of shared goals.
Instead of "this plan doesn’t make sense", say "how does this timeline match the user rollout expectations?" A calm,
respectful tone keeps the conversation focused and productive.

**Docs and writing:**
Documentation is often overlooked, but it’s a crucial part of any project. Review it with the same care you give code.
Look for clarity, structure, and usefulness. Don’t just say "this is unclear" — explain what part was confusing and why.
Suggest examples or rewordings. And always appreciate the effort — writing is hard.

**Team interactions and interpersonal feedback:**
This is the toughest kind. Telling someone their behavior is affecting others requires empathy and courage. Focus on
actions, not personality. For example: "In the last few meetings, we’ve run over time, and I noticed you’ve spoken the
most. Could we try to balance the airtime a bit more?" This kind of feedback keeps things objective and respectful.

## Helpful frameworks

When in doubt, you can lean on proven frameworks. They give structure to your feedback and help you avoid emotional
missteps.

**[Radical Candor](https://www.radicalcandor.com/)**
Kim Scott’s Radical Candor is about two things: care personally, and challenge directly. You want to be honest with your
feedback, but also show the person that you care about them. If you challenge without care, it feels like you're being
mean. If you care without challenging, you might become overly soft and not helpful. The goal is to be in the sweet spot
where you’re both kind and clear.

**[Nonviolent Communication (NVC)](https://www.cnvc.org/)**
Developed by Marshall Rosenberg, NVC is a method of expressing feedback without triggering defensiveness. It breaks
feedback into four parts:

* **Observation:** What happened, without judgment
* **Feeling:** How it made you feel
* **Need:** What you need or value
* **Request:** What you’d like to happen

Example: "I noticed the deployment script failed during testing. I felt stressed because I needed more time to prepare.
Could we review the test coverage together before the next deployment?"

It’s a respectful and clear way to address issues, especially when emotions are involved.

## When we mess up

We all get it wrong sometimes. Maybe you gave feedback that was too blunt. Maybe you ignored someone’s idea. Or maybe
you pushed your solution too hard. It happens.

What matters is how we respond. The best thing to do is acknowledge it, apologize if needed, and learn from it.

A good apology usually includes:

* A clear acknowledgment of what happened
* A statement of regret
* Acceptance of responsibility
* A plan or commitment to do better

For example: "I realize I dismissed your idea too quickly in the meeting. That wasn’t fair. I’ll make sure to listen
more carefully moving forward."

This kind of accountability strengthens trust. It shows you’re open to feedback too, and that you take the team
seriously.

## It’s about the team, not just you or me

The ultimate goal of reviews — code or not — is not to win debates or show who’s smarter. It’s to make the work better.
And to do that, we need to think about the team as a whole.

A few principles that help:

* **Assume good intent.** Most people want to do a good job. Start from that assumption.
* **Disagree and commit.** Once a decision is made, support it even if you had a different view.
* **Make space for everyone.** Encourage quieter team members to share. Use structured formats if needed.
* **Celebrate good reviews.** Praise team members who give kind, helpful feedback.

And don’t forget that how we review and respond to each other builds the culture. If we make reviews respectful, honest,
and safe, we create a space where everyone wants to contribute.

Let’s make feedback something people appreciate, not avoid. Let’s make it part of how we grow together — one thoughtful,
constructive review at a time.
